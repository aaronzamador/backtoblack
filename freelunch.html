Free Lunch redesign

Jump to the final designs

Project scope: User research, content strategy, data analysis, card sorting, information architecture, personas, wireframing, usability testing, stakeholder interview, UI design, responsive design

Duration: 3 weeks
Team size: 3
My role: Website UX and visual design

“Our big focus is obviously on healthy food, but also boosting morale. It’s gnarly out there.”

— Jazz Mills, founder of Free Lunch

A little background

Free Lunch ATX is a small business based in Austin, Texas, focused on tackling food insecurity for the local unhoused community. They help people experiencing homelessness find nourishing, healthy, and low-waste meals, fully funded by their Lunch Monitors–monthly supporters of their quarterly magazine.

The goal

Increase monthly supporters by redesigning the Free Lunch Website. Provide an optional supplementary app to quickly locate and access free meals for themselves or others struggling with food insecurity.

See the As-Is below.

Survey results

32 participants, age ranging 18-56.

How are users finding Free Lunch? Can their audience find them? Our survey aimed to understand how the unhoused community were able to access food.

Most participants were close to someone experiencing homelessness

64% of our participants knew someone experiencing some form of homelessness, and 22% had experienced it themselves.

The unhoused community count on people they know

100% of participants who had experienced homelessness had found access to food through their family, 70% found access through friends.

Most people experiencing homelessness access to smartphones

90% of the participants that had experienced a form of homelessness had access to a smartphone. 80% of participants that knew someone experiencing homelessness believed that the person was able to access a smartphone.

These findings meant a Free Lunch redesign was more important than ever. The unhoused community relied on these services to survive, so it needed to be informative and usable.

Usability testing

We needed to watch real people use the site to see what was working, and what wasn't.

  • Users didn't fully understand their mission.

  • They struggled finding where to get the free meals.

  • Users didn't understand why the only way to contribute was a recurring monthly donation.

  • Users found the websites' current styling unorganized and hard to read.

User persona

My teammates and I created an empathy map using the survey and interview results. That gave us a clearer picture of who they were, we called them Melissa Terry.

Melissa Terry is someone looking to help her community by donating her time and money. She's health conscious and cares about sustainability. She is familiar with the homeless community in her neighborhood, so this cause hits close to home.

Competitor analysis

We reviewed two like-minded nonprofits–Mobile Loaves and Fishes and the Food is Free Project–who we considered direct competitors of Free Lunch, and two indirect competitors–Caritas and Meals on Wheels.

Mobile Loaves and Fishes are well established, and they offer not only food but clothing, hygiene and other life-sustaining items. As a religious nonprofit they alienate a fraction of their potential supporters. The Food is Free Project provides homeowners resources to help them start their own sustainable food garden. Anyone can participate–if you own a patch of land.

Caritas of Austin has a responsive website that gives a lot of donation options, but navigating the website is difficult due to the large number of resources they offer and poor information architecture. Meals on Wheels, like Mobile Loaves and Fishes is well established and offers a wide range of services, but their pages can be a bit dull and text heavy. Caritas and Meals on Wheels both cover a broad scope of services, which seems to water down their efficacy according to reviews left online.

Ideation

We did an ideation exercise to identify possible pain relievers.

FL is ran by a group of Austin creatives–a film photographer, a musician, and an artist–so we not only wanted the website to have other donation options but also a shop that could sell these creatives’ projects and provide another source of income for FL.

We thought a mobile app for their supporters could expand their reach. It could enable users to send meals to others or reserve a meal that they could go pick up at a FL location.

Big ideas

  • Ability to create an account to manage monthly donation and to access the online Zine.

  • Online shop creation to increase methods of contribution.

  • Send meal feature so users could supply food to someone they knew in need.

  • Ability for user in need to reserve meals from a pickup location.

User flow and site map

We would be redesigning the Free Lunch website and designing a mobile app so my teammates and I drafted a user flow for both experiences and site map for the website. For the site map, we needed to correct the information architecture so users understand the Free Lunchs' mission and how to get involved, so we added an "About" page. We also added an "Account" page for the user to access their online Zine.

User journey

We sought pains and opportunities of the user donation process and their zine access for the website and the mobile app.

Goals

  • Provide a meal for someone she knows in her community that is in need.

  • Contribute to the cause by making a donation.

Opportunities

  • Provide sufficient and similar information across both experiences to prevent confusion.

  • Multiple payment options and instant access to their Zine.

Wireframes

We wanted to make the Free Lunch website more accessible to their audience thereby increase their funding. We delegated my the mobile app to my two teammates while I focused on the website.

They brought mobile app sketches that we chose the best features from, focusing on the task of finding and reserving a nearby meal, sending a meal to someone in need, or reading the online Zine. My website sketches focused on implementing this and including a shopping component, newsletter subscription, and donation goal tracker. We collaborated by translated these sketches into lo-fi wireframes.

Stakeholder interview

We spoke with Free Lunch Founder Jazz Mills early in the project, but were not able to speak in depth until after our research, ideating and sketches. She told us their operation is relatively small, and they prefer to stay focused rather than spreading themselves too thin.

"Our program isn't trying to solve homelessness in Austin – that's not realistic for us...We just want to tackle food insecurity at Camp Esperanza, and then at another camp and another. What really matters is that when we tell people we're going to bring them food, we don't bail."

— Jazz Mills, founder of Free Lunch

Key takeaways

  • Free Lunch is focused on quality of their service over quantity of camps served.

  • Small operation that needs more supporters to grow.

  • Their magazine subscription is the only form of income they prefer to pursue.

  • Free Lunch aims to avoid large one time donations because they know it means inconsistent funding.

Rescoping

The founder of Free Lunch, gave us insights into their operations and goals. For Free Lunch, we were too ambitious and needed to scale back. Jazz offered ideas into what she thought would be realistic, like recipes for those looking to contribute immediately, and other resources they prefer to promote.

Luckily, our end goal hadn't changed–Redesign the Free Lunch experience so the mission and goals are clear and they look credbile, increase monthly supporters and provide (a now optional) supplementary app to quickly locate and access free meals should they expand.

Mobile app - 5 year plan

  • Operate under assumption in five years they would have multiple locations for picking up reserved meals.

  • Remove donation and nearby meals feature.

  • Ability to access Zine subscription.

  • Ability to reserve a free meal at a FL location.

Website changes

  • Remove one time donation option and nearby meals feature.

  • Replace shop feature with feature highlighting local screenprinting company they partner with.

  • Clarify their mission as funded directly through their magazine subscriptions.

Testing

Free Lunch at this time, wouldn't need the mobile app, but we continued to work with the concept for the challenge. We made adjustments to our existing wireframes tested that users be able to understand Free Lunch's mission and get subscribed to the Zine.

Users identified the Free Lunch mission

With clearer context of the way Free Lunch operated, users reported back to us their mission, hitting all three of the marks we were looking for–healthy, and sustainable, magazine subscription.

“Wow I think this is such a good idea, I wish there were more people out there doing this.”

Users were confused about their subscription status

Utilizing the new "Account" page we implemented and signing up to their account seemed to be a breeze. When asked to subscribe to the Zine and become a Lunch Monitor, users hesitated after entering their card information. They didn't know if it worked. To address this we iterated a confirmation screen.

“Alright, so I'll press subscribe, and.... Did it work?”

Hi-Fi screens

We delegated app wireframes to my teammates, I worked the style guide and hi-fi's for the website. Free Lunch had a vivid Instagram presence, so I pulled design cues from their profiles to jazz up the website redesign. A focus on big food textures, fresh and bright colors, and local, organic energy that could capture their mission and the sensibilities of Austin.

I aimed for bold, punchy, and impactful typography, with clear and compelling call to actions.

❮ ❯

Final thoughts

Loved the experience working directly with our stakeholder, Jazz. I would like to revisit our user Melissa Terry after another interview with Jazz. The mobile app designs are shelved but available for her when they are ready. I'm proud to support Free Lunch in their continued their efforts to feed those in need, so if you're local to Austin, check them out!
