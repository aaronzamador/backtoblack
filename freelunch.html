Free Lunch redesign

Jump to the final designs

Project scope: User research, content strategy, data analysis, card sorting, information architecture, personas, wireframing, usability testing, stakeholder interview, UI design, responsive design

Duration: 3 weeks
Team size: 3
My role: Website UX and visual design

“Our big focus is obviously on healthy food, but also boosting morale. It’s gnarly out there.”

— Jazz Mills, founder of Free Lunch

A little background

Free Lunch ATX is a small business based in Austin, Texas, focused on tackling food insecurity for the local unhoused community. They were created to help people experiencing homelessness find nourishing, healthy, and low-waste meals. They're fully funded by their Lunch Monitors–monthly supporters of their quarterly magazine.

The goal

Increase monthly supporters by redesigning the Free Lunch Website so that visitors can clearly understand Free Lunchs' mission and how to get involved. Eventually implement a supplementary app to quickly locate and access free meals for themselves or others struggling with food insecurity.

See the As-Is and To-Be below.

Survey results

We started our redesign with a survey that we sent to 30 participants.

How are users finding them? Can their audience find them? Our survey aimed to understand how the unhoused community were able to access food.

Most participants were close to someone experiencing homelessness

64% of our participants knew someone experiencing some form of homelessness, and 22% had experienced it themselves.

The unhoused community count on people they know

100% of participants who had experienced homelessness had found access to food through their family, 70% found access through friends.

Most people experiencing homelessness access to smartphones

90% of the participants that had experienced a form of homelessness had access to a smartphone. 80% of participants that knew someone experiencing homelessness believed that the person was able to access a smartphone.

These findings meant a Free Lunch redesign was more important than ever. The unhoused community relied on these services to survive, so it needed to be informative and usable.

Usability testing

We needed to know from real users what was working, and what wasn't.

  • Users didn't fully understand their mission.

  • They struggled finding where to go to access free meals.

  • Users didn't understand why the only way to contribute was a recurring monthly donation.

  • Users found the websites' current styling unorganized and hard to read.

User persona

My teammates and I created an empathy map for our user. We synthesized our survey and interview results to understand what our user was thinking and feeling.

Our user, Melissa Terry, is someone looking to help her community by donating her time and money. She has friends who are experiencing homelessness, so this cause hits close to home. She's health conscious and cares about sustainability.

Competitor analysis

My teammates and I decided to review two nonprofits–Mobile Loaves and Fishes and the Food is Free Project–who we considered direct competitors of Free Lunch, and two indirect competitors–Caritas and Meals on Wheels.

Mobile Loaves and Fishes has been around for 21 years, and they offer not only food but clothing, hygiene and other life-sustaining items. As a religious nonprofit they alienate a fraction of their potential supporters. The Food is Free Project provides homeowners resources to help them start their own sustainable food garden. Anyone can participate–if you own a patch of land.

Caritas of Austin has a responsive website that gives a lot of donation options, but navigating the website is difficult due to the large number of resources they offer and poor information architecture. Meals on Wheels, like Mobile Loaves and Fishes is well established and offers a wide range of services, but their pages can be a bit dull and text heavy. Caritas and Meals on Wheels both cover a broad scope of services, which seems to water down their efficacy according to reviews left online.

Ideation and brainstorming

My teammates and I did an ideation exercise to identify possible pain relievers.

FL is ran by a group of Austin creatives–a film photographer, a musician, and an artist–so we not only wanted the website to have other donation options but also a shop that could sell these creatives’ projects and provide another source of income for FL.

We wanted to expand their reach and ease of access by creating a mobile app for their supporters that would allow users to send meals to others or reserve a meal they could go pick up at a FL location.

Primary features

  • Ability to create an account to manage monthly donation and to access their online Zine.

  • Online shop creation to increase methods of contribution.

  • Send meal feature so that users could supply food to someone they knew in need.

  • Ability for user in need to reserve meals from a pickup location.

User flow and site map

We would be redesigning the Free Lunch website and designing a mobile app. My teammates and I drafted a user flow for both experiences and site map for the website. For the site map, we knew we needed to better organize the information so that users could clearly understand Free Lunchs' mission and how to get involved, so we added an "About" page. We also added an "Account" page for the user to access their online Zine.

User journey

We created a user journey for both the website and the mobile app as a way to visualize pains and opportunities our user would experience while navigating the application and site.

Goals

  • Provide a meal for someone she knows in her community that is in need.

  • Contribute to the cause by making a donation.

Opportunities

  • Provide sufficient and similar information across both experiences to prevent confusion.

  • Multiple payment options and instant access to their Zine.

Wireframes

My teammates and I looked at the mobile app and the website redesign as a big opportunity for us to make Free Lunch more accessible to their audience and increase their funding. After spending a lot of time considering the user's flow through our designs, we decided to split up to divide and conquer the two experiences. My two teammates decided to tackle the mobile app while I focused on the website.

The mobile app sketches focused on the features we ideated–finding and reserving a nearby meal, sending a meal to someone in need, or reading the online Zine. My website sketches focused on implementing these new features too, as well as adding the shop section and other methods of increasing engagement such as a newsletter subscription and donation goal tracker. We translated these sketches into lo-fi wireframes once we had discussed how we felt about the direction we were heading.

Stakeholder interview

We connected through email with Free Lunch Founder Jazz Mills early in our design project, but due to scheduling constraints we were not able to speak with her until the end of week two, after our research, ideating and sketches. She told us their operation is relatively small, and they prefer to stay focused rather than spreading themselves too thin.

"Our program isn't trying to solve homelessness in Austin – that's not realistic for us...We just want to tackle food insecurity at Camp Esperanza, and then at another camp and another. What really matters is that when we tell people we're going to bring them food, we don't bail."

— Jazz Mills, founder of Free Lunch

Key takeaways

  • Free Lunch is focused on quality of their service over quantity of camps served.

  • Small operation that needs more supporters to grow.

  • Their magazine subscription is the only form of income they prefer to pursue.

  • Free Lunch aims to avoid large one time donations because they know it means inconsistent funding.

Rescoping

Speaking with Jazz Mills, the founder of Free Lunch,gave us a lot of insights into their day to day operations and goals. For Free Lunch, many of our initial ideas were too ambitious, so we needed to reconsider how we planned on getting to our end goal. Jazz offered ideas into what she thought would be realistic, like recipes for those looking to contribute immediately, and other resources they prefer to promote.

Luckily, our end goal hadn't changed–Redesign the Free Lunch experience so that users clearly understand their mission and goals, build a stronger connection between their website and other social medias, and eventually implement a supplementary app to quickly locate and access free meals (we're talking three to five years down the line).

Mobile app - 5 year plan

  • Operate under assumption in five years they would have multiple locations for picking up reserved meals.

  • Remove donation and nearby meals feature.

Website changes

  • Remove one time donation options and nearby meals feature.

  • Replace shop feature with feature highlighting local screenprinting company they partner with.

  • Clarify their mission as funded directly through their magazine subscriptions.

Testing

In order to align with Free Lunchs' current scope, the mobile app wouldn't be needed. Instead, we made a few adjustments to our existing website wireframes and put them to the test. It was important to us that users be able to understand Free Lunch's mission and get subscribed to the Zine.

Users quickly identified the Free Lunch mission

With clearer context of the way Free Lunch operated, users reported back to us their mission, hitting all three of the marks we were looking for–healthy, and sustainable, magazine subscription.

“Wow I think this is such a good idea, I wish there were more people out there doing this.”

Users were confused about their subscription status

Utilizing the new "Account" page we implemented and signing up to their account seemed to be a breeze. When asked to subscribe to the Zine and become a Lunch Monitor, users hesitated after entering their card information. They didn't know if it worked. To address this we iterated a confirmation screen.

“Alright, so I'll press subscribe, and.... Did it work?”

Hi-Fi screens

The mobile app design was my teammates responsibility, the website designs were mine. Free Lunch had an amazing Instagram presence, but their personality wasn't coming through on their website. Their existing website had no photos of the staff, so I wanted to avoid using staff in photos, instead focusing on big food textures. I wanted an organic, poster style that I felt captured their 'local/film/creative' personality.
