See-thru mobile app design

Project scope: User research, data analysis, personas, wireframing, usability testing, Product design, UI design

Duration: 2 weeks as a team, 2 weeks solo
Team size: 2

“Our mission is to promote student achievement and preparation for global competitiveness by fostering educational excellence and ensuring equal access.”

— U.S. Department of Education

The problem

The global pandemic has changed how we imagine dining out. Restaurants are not making their safety standards and dining options clear, causing travelers who are high risk to avoid eating out. How might we provide all safety and dining information clearly so that high risk travelers will feel safe to dine out during the pandemic?

The proposition

See-Thru is an app to help health concerned travelers make informed eating decisions during the Covid19 pandemic. See Thru takes the stress out of discovering local restaurants. Explore their menu, reviews and safety values all in one place. We follow up with recent diners to keep the information transparent, up to date, and real.

User interviews

My teammate and I wanted to uncover how travelers were dealing with the Coronavirus–what new concerns they have while eating out in a foreign place, and how their decisions could be made quicker and easier. We interviewed six users who had traveled pre-pandemic and had interest in traveling post-pandemic.

Before the pandemic

  • Users considered trip planning easy and fun.

  • Time spent varied, but users expressed excitement over their choices and adventures.

During the pandemic

  • User's spent a lot more time researching their trips, but still didn't know what to expect.

  • Users wanted the same quality of adventure, but cleaner and safer.

User personas

After analyzing insights from our user interviews, my teammate and I began an empathy map for our new persona. We wanted to jot down what our user was thinking and feeling to guide us into our user persona.

Users visit restaurants to have an enjoyable dining experience, and they expect that the restaurant maintains health standards for all eaters. My teammate and I observed that restaurants are not making our health-conscious travelers feel safe and are not providing accurate and complete information online about their safety standards, which is causing high risk travelers to avoid eating there.

Information architecture analysis

Our users made valid points–a lot of the content they were looking for didn’t seem to fit into one of the four navigation options offered, it was difficult to locate in long pages of text, and deciphering ED’s code took considerable time and effort. With this valuable feedback in mind, we made it our mission to break down why these were issues. We experimented with a card sorting exercise to visualize the Department’s current information architecture so that we could better grasp how we might improve it.

Viewing the website’s content this way brought a few key points to our attention.

Some pages were hidden

Beyond the primary navigation, we found a hidden page–Top Tasks–filled with a collection of important links. It’s great for browsing, but many of the links can’t be found anywhere else on the site. We wondered if there was a way to integrate these useful links into the site’s main navigation.

Page titles were indescriptive

Imagine you’re a parent to a child with disabilities searching for the legal rights that protect your child from discrimination, would you visit Parental Rights or What are my Rights? I’m betting parents spend a lot of time juggling that decision.

Every page was link overload

In lieu of creating an adequate navigation, every page on the website is filled with links heading somewhere else. Why would a parent interested in student loans need a link to Constitution Day? It all just seems like a band-aid approach to avoid reconsidering their navigation.

With these three points in mind, and what we had discovered from user testing, we created a new site map. The goal was to better group similar topics, reduce the amount of similarly named pages, and add new navigation for pages that didn’t seem to belong anywhere else.

Wireframes

Begin week three, our team divided to continue the project solo. With the IA carefully reconsidered, it was time to turn my focus to where it would live. My goal hadn’t changed, reimagine how visitors were expected to find what they need. Make the experience easy, responsive and reassuring.

My sketches revealed a clean, modular design for ED that I felt confident could address many problems that our team had uncovered. Digital wireframes came with more things to consider, and more ways to address our user’s needs.

  • A consistent layout that could be used by someone using either a phone or a desktop.

  • Bolder hierarchy and clearer indication of where in the website you were.

  • Two new items and a search bar were added to the navigation.

  • ED’s content and voice was reconsidered to remove jargen and give user’s clear direction.

Testing the wireframes

I needed to be sure I was headed in the right direction, so I tested new users with the same original tasks, basic functions of the website and the user’s ability to locate and understand important details about aid.

Time per task was dramatically reduced.

Users still seemed to prefer using the search bar, however finding it not usable at this stage of the prototype, were still able to find what they needed without it relatively quickly.

“Isn’t there a way to show me everything I need at once?”

–User 2, a mother of two

Users expressed frustration over flipping through pages

While relatively easy, several users noted that they wished they could read about more than one topic at a time.

“I wish I didn’t have to click back and forth through these tabs every time.”

–User 1, a mother of two

One last feature

I spent some time considering the last thoughts my users had left me with. Inspired by their frustration, and empowered by my modular design, I reimagined ED’s experience one last time.

Filter by tag

Users wanted to see more than one topic at the same time, and were tired of flipping through tabs. The landing page was iterated to invite the visitor to select topics they were interested in reading about so that they could view more content at once if they chose. The tabs were left for their convenience.

Style guide

To address many issues discovered early on during our heuristic analysis, I designed a style guide. Components and icons were created to prevent inconsistency, new colors were chosen and retested against usability guidelines, and ED’s tone and brand began to take shape.

My final thoughts

Redesigning ED.gov was a passion project that I was personally invested in, as a previous user of their site. An important lesson I learned while taking on this challenge was the importance of a non-linear design flow. What shocked me about this project was how even to the last minute, users were still identifying ways it could be better. Allowing myself the freedom to iterate is how I was able to truly meet all of the user’s needs. What I finished with is something I truly think would create a quick and easy, informative trip to the Department’s website.
