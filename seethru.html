See-Thru mobile app design

Jump to the final designs

Project scope: User research, data analysis, personas, wireframing, usability testing, Product design, UI design

Duration: 2 weeks as a team, 2 weeks solo
Team size: 2
My role: UX and visual design

“Our mission is to promote student achievement and preparation for global competitiveness by fostering educational excellence and ensuring equal access.”

— U.S. Department of Education

The problem

The global pandemic changed how we dine out. Restaurants rarely make their safety standards and dining options clear, so travelers avoid eating out. How might we make safety and dining information clear while bring the fun back to dining during the pandemic?

The proposition

See-Thru is an app to help travelers make informed eating decisions during the Covid-19 pandemic. See Thru offers local restaurants based on your safety preferences. Explore their menu, reviews and safety values all in one place. Recent diners verify to keep the information transparent, up to date, and real.

See the To-Be below.

User interviews

My teammate and I wanted to uncover how travelers were dealing with the Coronavirus–what new concerns they have while dining out, and what pains do they go through? We interviewed six users who had traveled pre-pandemic and had interest in traveling during the pandemic

Before the pandemic

  • Users considered trip planning easy, fun, and not too time consuming.

  • Time spent varied, but users expressed excitement over their choices and adventures.

During the pandemic

  • User's spent a lot more time researching their trips, but still didn't know what to expect.

  • Users wanted the same quality of adventure, but cleaner and safer.

User persona

We made an empathy map for our user Laurel, informed by our user interviews, then created a persona for them.

We concluded someone like Laurel would visit restaurants to have an enjoyable dining experience, and they would expect the restaurant maintain health standards. We observed that restaurants are not making travelers feel safe and are not providing accurate and/or complete information online about their safety standards, which is causing travelers to avoid eating there.

Pains

  • Must blindly trust restaurants are doing their part to keep patrons safe.

  • Uninformed of all the safety measures in place.

  • May spend a lot of time researching information that may not be correct.

  • Users miss out on an enjoyable dining experience.

Gains

  • Accurate and updated information shown before arriving.

  • Knowing how busy a restaurant is prior to arriving.
  • Visible safety information and dining options.

  • Reduce the risk of getting sick.

Competitor analysis

We reviewed three companies–Yelp, OpenTable, and FourSquare–who we considered direct competitors of our potential app, and two indirect competitors–Reddit and Instagram.

Yelp, Open Table, and Foursquare all give options for discovering, reading and leaving reviews, and seeing multiple dining options. Instagram and Reddit are great mediums for finding recommendations, but they do not allow for interactive menus, have limited dining information, and have no reservation options.

Ideation and brainstorming

My partner and I individually completed an 'I like, I wish, What if?' exercise to get our brain juices flowing. We collaborated by sharing and grouping our similar ideas. My ideas are visualized below in the lighter colors, my teammate's in the darker.

Solo, I reviewed all the ideas prioritized what would be best for Laurel, have a significant impact and be feasible for my product.

Primary features

  • Curate restaurant options based on a user's selected safety preferences.

  • Display a restaurant's enforced safety precautions.

  • Real time feedback about a restaurant's safety standards from a previous eaters.

  • Allow for orders or reservations to be placed.

User journey

I created a user journey as a way to visualize pains and opportunities my user would experience while navigating my application.

Goals

  • Have enjoyable experience dining out during the pandemic.

  • Provide enough accurate research before hand.

Opportunities

  • An onboarding experience that is comprehensive and useful without overwhelming the user.

  • An intellegent AI that gathers data from multiple web sources to generate real data about the restaurant for users.

Storyboarding

For context of Laurel's journey, I created a storyboard

Laurel is going on a trip

Laurel and her husband have decided they will take trip. She spends time excitedly planning places she would like to visit.

She's hungry in a new area

Laurel and her husband are in Chicago, they begin to wonder where they might want to eat. Laurel is high risk for Covid-19 and they both prefer to wear masks, keep their distance and choose somewhere that is taking steps to keep their patrons safe.

She tries to do some research online

Laurel browses the web, first searching for top places to eat in Chicago. Then switches to Reddit for local recommendations. She plugs those places into Maps to find out how close it is to her. Maps has vague information on hours and menu, but she’s had experience with that being wrong before. The many resources she has to use is overwhelming for her.

Laurel discovers See-Thru

Laurel finds an ad for See-Thru. She downloads the app and is happy to see she can put in a location and filter by distance from her. The app curates local favorites she can browse through, that have up to date menus, hours, and dining options. The safety measures being taken are also clear. She scrolls through options to see reviews that were left by recent diners. This makes her feel confident in her choice.

Laurel makes an informed decision

Laurel chooses a restaurant close by, that shows they are taking proper precautions and have space for her to eat in. In App she reserves a table for the two of them. She is excited because the locals have been raving about this place.

Laurel arrives, enjoys, and confirms.

Laurel and her husband arrive at the restaurant, pleased to see the safety precautions listed in the app were correct. Her reservation was received so the two of them are seated quickly, avoiding crowds. Her experience is exactly what she expected. After her meal, she logs back into See-Thru, and completes the follow up survey, helping out the next potential eater.

Wireframes

I sketched rapidly many iterations of the app until I had something to work with.

My sketches revealed a lightweight, familiar design for See-Thru which I translated into digital wireframes.

  • Meal search and discovery with built in order or reservation options.

  • Safety preference filters a user may optionally complete during onboarding.

  • A safety match that rates restaurants on how they align with your safety preferences.

  • Clear safety standards that are in place with user ratings to validate.

Testing the wireframes

Could users sign up? Did they want to complete the preferences during onboarding? If not, could they find out how to skip them? Could they schedule a reservation at a restaurant?

Users were hesitant about allowing location services during onboarding.

While going through my onboarding process, every single user expressed their frustration over allowing location services. I decided to move the location services permission modal further into the app experience to catch the user in context.

“I don't like to give my location, especially when I'm not even trying to do something that needs my location right now.”

Users were confused about the dropdown multi-select during onboarding

Every user struggled with understanding the dropdown multi-select I had implemented into the onboarding process for selecting safety preferences. I decided to change this component to a button select method.

“Can I only choose one of these?”

I further analyzed and prioritized the feedback from my user tests to be sure I didn't miss any opportunities in completing the See-Thru flow.

Final thoughts
