Ed.gov redesign

Jump to the final designs

Check out the finished prototype

Project scope: User research, content strategy, data analysis, card sorting, information architecture, personas, style guide, wireframing, usability testing, UI design, responsive design

Duration: 2 weeks as a team, 3 weeks solo
Team size: 4
My role: UX and visual design

“Our mission is to promote student achievement and preparation for global competitiveness by fostering educational excellence and ensuring equal access.”

— U.S. Department of Education

A little background

The Department of Education's website recieved 15 million hits in 2019, users ranging from students and parents, to educators, stakeholders, and officials. The Department is equipped with a budget of $68 billion, and more than 4,400 employees.

So then why, with a mission statement boasting student achievement and equal access for all, would their website fail to meet usability standards that were released seven years prior to their last large-scale update in 2015? How can we improve our fellow citizen’s experience navigating this valuable resource so that they can easily take advantage of all the resources our nation has to offer?

The goal

Improve visitors experience finding and understanding information they need on the ED website.

See the As-Is and To-Be below.

A heuristic review

Many aspects of the site–the navigation, layout, IA, accessibility, tone–all needed work.

  • Several components are inconsistent depending on the page you’re viewing.

  • The site provides no feedback, and no indication of where you are.

  • Many color combinations are inaccessible by WCAG 2.0 guidelines.

  • The Department fails to portray a clear voice or brand.

Testing with users

We created a testing plan for a focused persona of a subsection of the many potential users.

Parents make up 40% of the U.S. population in 2020, so we focused on their experience using ED. We tested their ability to find and use the navigation, search bar, and contact information. Then we tested their ability to locate and understand important details about federal aid–the different types of loans, their eligibility for aid, and scholarships that were currently available.

Users preferred the search bar over the main navigation

The existing navigation is purposefully simple, however the majority of our users decided to use the search bar.

Find a scholarship? Is that a loan or a grant? I'm just gonna use the search bar.”

–User 2, a mother of two

Users revisited pages multiple times before finding what they were looking for

After using the search bar or primary navigation, users frequently came to the right page the first time, but they didn’t know it. Nail in the coffin? The majority of our users blamed themselves.

I can't believe it was here the whole time, I'm seriously blind!”

–User 1, a mother of two

Many users simply didn’t understand the content

When users did succeed in finding the right information, many found themselves feeling frustrated in their attempt to make sense of ED’s jargon.

Well, I kind of get it more after reading the second time.. Wait, let me read it again.”

–User 5, father of one

Information architecture analysis

We analyzed the Department's current information architecture by card sorting their existing primary and sub navigation.

Some pages were hidden

Beyond the primary navigation, we found a hidden page "Top Tasks". It’s great for browsing, but many of the links can’t be found anywhere else on the site. We wanted to integrate these useful links into the site’s main navigation.

Page titles were vague

Imagine you’re a parent to a child with disabilities searching for the legal rights that protect your child from discrimination, would you visit Parental Rights or What are my Rights? I’m betting parents spend a lot of time juggling that decision.

Every page was link overload

In lieu of creating an adequate navigation, every page on the website is filled with links heading somewhere else. Why would a parent interested in student loans need a link to Constitution Day?

With these three points in mind, and what we had discovered from user testing, we created a new site map. The goal was to better group similar topics, reduce the amount of similarly named pages, and add new navigation for pages that didn’t seem to belong anywhere else.

❮ ❯

Wireframes

Week three our team divided to continue the project solo and I sketched ideas for the interface. My goal was to improve visitors experience finding and understanding information on ED's website. Make the experience easy, responsive and reassuring.

I created a series of wireframes and midfis as I continued to develop the final design.

❮ ❯

  • A consistent layout that could be used by someone using either a phone or a desktop.

  • Bolder hierarchy and clearer indication of where in the website you were.

  • Two new items and a search bar were added to the navigation.

  • ED’s content and voice was reconsidered to remove jargen and give user’s clear direction.

Testing wireframes

Six new users were asked to use basic functions of the website and locate and understand important details about aid.

Time per task was dramatically reduced.

Users still seemed to prefer using the search bar, however finding it not usable at this stage of the prototype, were still able to find what they needed without it relatively quickly.

“Isn’t there a way to show me everything I need at once?”

–User 2, a mother of two

Users expressed frustration over scrolling a long single page.

Several users noted that they wished they could read about more than one topic at a time without seeing what they didn't need.

“I wish I didn’t have to scroll back and forth through this long page.”

–User 1, a mother of two

One last feature

I considered the final thoughts my users left me with and iterated ED’s experience one last time.

Filter by tag

Users wanted to see more than one topic at the same time, and were tired of flipping through tabs. The landing page was iterated to invite the visitor to select topics they were interested in reading about so that they could view more content at once if they chose. The tabs were left for their convenience.

Style guide

Next I designed a style guide. Components and icons were created to prevent inconsistency, new colors were chosen and retested against usability guidelines, and ED’s tone and brand began to take shape.

Final thoughts

I loved working with a site I've personally had to deal with. My final rounds of testing show if I were to revisit this project I still have work to do with the information architecture. I'd love to continue fleshing out the remaining pages and there are a lot of components I'd like to reimagine.
